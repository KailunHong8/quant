Yes. *Principles of Corporate Finance* (Brealey–Myers–Allen) is one of the core textbooks in modern finance.  Its ideas map very well to trading if you translate “corporate projects” into “trades/strategies” and “shareholder value” into “your risk‑adjusted equity curve.”[1][2][3][4]

Below is a compact framework you can use while reading, plus how to turn each concept into something actionable for your 2026 trading.

***

## Core ideas of the book

### Goal and time value of money

The book starts from two foundations:  
1. **Goal of finance:** maximize shareholder wealth (market value of equity), not accounting profit.[2][3]
2. **Time value of money:** a euro today is worth more than a euro tomorrow; all decisions should be based on discounted cash flows (DCF).[5][2]

For you as a trader:  
- Replace “shareholder wealth” with **your account value**. Every strategy should be judged by its effect on **risk‑adjusted account growth**, not by how “often it wins.”  
- Think of any trade as a **project** with cash flows over time (fees, margin interest, possible P&L paths), then ask: does the *expected* discounted payoff justify the capital at risk?

***

### Risk, return, and CAPM (beta vs idiosyncratic risk)

Brealey treats **risk and return** as the central link between markets and corporate decisions.  Key points:[6][7][2]
- Investors care about **expected return** and **variance** (volatility) of return.  
- In equilibrium, only **systematic risk** (market risk) is rewarded; **idiosyncratic risk** can be diversified away and should not earn extra return.[7][8]
- CAPM gives the basic pricing relation:  
  \(\text{Expected return} = r_f + \beta (R_m - r_f)\)  
  where \(\beta\) measures how sensitive an asset is to the market.[9][7]

For your trading:  
- Treat beta as **“how much this trade loads on macro risk factors”**. A high‑beta tech stock or EM FX carry trade is like taking leverage on the global risk‑on/risk‑off cycle.  
- Don’t waste risk budget on stuff that is just **idiosyncratic noise** with no clear edge—Brealey would tell you that only systematic exposures should earn a risk premium; idiosyncratic bets must be backed by genuine information/alpha.[8][6]
- Use a CAPM mindset as a **sanity check**: if your strategy’s backtested return is only slightly above the CAPM‑implied return for its beta, it’s probably not worth the stress and drawdowns.

***

### Capital budgeting = trade selection

In the book, firms choose projects by tools like **NPV (Net Present Value)** and **IRR (Internal Rate of Return)**.[3][10][2]
- **NPV rule:** accept projects with positive NPV (expected value > 0 after discounting at the opportunity cost of capital).  
- **Risk‑adjusted discount rate:** riskier cash flows must be discounted at a higher required return.[11][10]

For you, think of each **trading system / setup** as a project:  
- Your “cash flows” are the **distribution of P&L over time** plus costs (commissions, slippage, borrowing).  
- Your “discount rate” is your **required return** for that level of risk (e.g., “for a strategy with 20% annual vol, I require at least 15% expected annual return”).  
- You should **only allocate real capital to strategies with positive “NPV”**: backtests and forward tests show an edge *after* realistic costs and drawdowns.  

A practical translation:  
- Build a list of candidate strategies for 2026 (e.g., global macro carry, volatility selling, gold trend‑following).  
- For each, estimate expected annual return, volatility, max drawdown, and correlation to your other strategies.  
- Apply a simple rule: *allocate more to strategies with the highest “excess return per unit of risk” (Sharpe / Sortino), and cut or size down those whose “NPV” looks marginal.*

***

### Capital structure and leverage

The book’s capital‑structure chapters analyze **debt vs equity**: how much leverage can a firm take before risk outweighs benefit.  Ideas you can reuse:[2][3]
- More leverage raises expected equity return **and** equity risk (variance).  
- There is a trade‑off: cheap debt benefits are offset by financial distress risk and loss of flexibility.  

For trading:  
- Your **account is the equity**, your **margin/borrowed money is the debt**. Brealey’s lesson: don’t chase slightly higher returns at the cost of a **non‑linear blow‑up risk**.  
- In 2026, with higher and more volatile real rates (Fu Peng’s “counter‑tide” logic), **tail events** are more common. Leverage should be conservative; think like a CFO who needs to keep the firm alive through stress scenarios.

***

### Risk management, options, and real options

Later chapters cover **options, hedging, and “real options”**—flexibility in investment decisions.[12][3][2]
- Options theory (Black–Scholes, put–call parity) explains how markets price **asymmetric payoffs**, not just linear exposures.  
- “Real options” show that having the **choice** to expand, delay, or abandon projects has value, especially in uncertain environments.[12][2]

Applied to your trading:  
- Think of **cash + optionality** as a strategic asset. In volatile 2026 markets, keeping dry powder to buy when others are forced sellers is a “real option.”  
- Use options to shape your payoff: e.g., replace a high‑leverage directional trade with **call spreads** or **put‑protected positions** so your downside is bounded. That is corporate‑style **risk management**, not just speculation.  

***

## How to turn the book into a 2026 trading roadmap

Here is a simple way to integrate Brealey with your practice:

1. **Define your objective the Brealey way**  
   - Write down: “My trading goal in 2026 is to maximize *risk‑adjusted* growth of my capital (like maximizing shareholder wealth), subject to a max drawdown tolerance of X%.”  
   - Everything else (frequency of trades, instruments, leverage) is subordinate to that.

2. **Map your current and planned strategies to corporate concepts**  
   - Each **strategy** = “project” (capital budgeting).  
   - Each **trade** = one small realisation of that project’s cash flows.  
   - Use your backtests/track record to estimate “NPV”: expected edge after costs vs required return for its risk.

3. **Impose a Brealey‑style capital allocation discipline**  
   - Rank strategies by **expected excess return / volatility** and correlation.  
   - Size positions so that the **portfolio beta** to global risk factors (equity indices, FX carry, rates) matches what you’re comfortable with.  
   - Treat leverage as **capital structure choice**: only use as much as you’d be comfortable seeing in a CFO’s presentation to the board.

4. **Use risk/return theory to avoid bad bets**  
   - Avoid strategies that simply add **idiosyncratic risk** without clear edge—Brealey would say you’re not being paid for that risk.[6][7]
   - Demand higher expected returns for trades that are heavily exposed to systematic risk (e.g., high beta crypto or EM risk) than for low‑beta, diversifying trades.

5. **Add “real options” to your process**  
   - Build rules for **when you have the option to stop** (cut strategy), **scale** (add capital when edge confirms), or **delay** (pause trading during policy/event risk) instead of forcing yourself to always be fully invested.  
   - View cash and T‑bills as a **strategic option**, not “wasted capital.”

If you like, the next step can be: you list your concrete strategies you’re planning for 2026 (e.g., gold carry, BTC swing, equity index options), and the explanation can be turned into a Brealey‑style “investment committee memo” for each strategy with clear risk/return, beta, and capital‑budgeting logic.
